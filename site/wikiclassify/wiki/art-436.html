}}</ref>==Philosophy==Plutarch was a [[Middle Platonism|Platonist]], but was open to the influence of the [[Peripatetics]], and in some details even to [[Stoicism]] despite his criticism of their principles lt;ref name="zeller306">Eduard Zeller, ''Outlines of the History of Greek Philosophy'', 13th edition, page 306</ref> He rejected only [[Epicureanism]] absolutely lt;ref name="zeller306"/> He attached little importance to theoretical questions and doubted the possibility of ever solving them lt;ref name="zeller307">Eduard Zeller, ''Outlines of the History of Greek Philosophy'', 13th edition, page 307</ref> He was more interested in moral and religious questions lt;ref name="zeller307"/>In opposition to Stoic materialism and Epicurean "atheism" he cherished a pure idea of [[God]] that was more in accordance with [[Plato]] lt;ref name="zeller307"/> He adopted a second principle (''[[Dyad (Greek philosophy)|Dyad]]'') in order to explain the phenomenal world lt;ref name="zeller307"/> This principle he sought, however, not in any indeterminate matter but in the evil world-soul which has from the beginning been bound up with matter, but in the creation was filled with reason and arranged by it lt;ref name="zeller307"/> Thus it was transformed into the divine soul of the world, but continued to operate as the source of all evil lt;ref name="zeller307"/> He elevated God above the finite world, and thus [[Daemon (mythology)|daemons]] became for him agents of God's influence on the world He strongly defends freedom of the will, and the immortality of the soul lt;ref name="zeller307"/>Platonic-Peripatetic [[ethics]] were upheld by Plutarch against the opposing theories of the Stoics and Epicureans lt;ref name="zeller307"/> The most characteristic feature of Plutarch's ethics is, however, its close connection with religion lt;ref name="zeller308">Eduard Zeller, ''Outlines of the History of Greek Philosophy'', 13th edition, page 308</ref> However pure Plutarch's idea of God is, and however vivid his description of the vice and corruption which superstition causes, his warm religious feelings and his distrust of human powers of knowledge led him to believe that God comes to our aid by direct revelations, which we perceive the more clearly the more completely that we refrain in "enthusiasm" from all action; this made it possible for him to justify popular belief in [[divination]] in the way which had long been usual among the Stoics lt;ref name="zeller308"/>His attitude to popular religion was similar The gods of different peoples are merely different names for one and the same divine Being and the powers that serve it lt;ref name="zeller308"/> The [[Greek mythology|myths]] contain philosophical truths which can be interpreted allegorically lt;ref name="zeller308"/> Thus Plutarch sought to combine the philosophical and religious conception of things and to remain as close as possible to tradition lt;ref

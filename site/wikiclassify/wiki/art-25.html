pg|thumb|right|275px|Abraham's well at Beersheba]][[Historical criticism]] has largely dismissed Abraham's purported historicity The 1906 ''[[Jewish Encyclopedia]]'' already concluded that Abraham's biography in Genesis should be regarded as legendary Its authors noted that the Elamite invasion of Genesis 14 may be historical and that Abraham's wandering may represent some historical tribal migration, but added that "[b]y reason of the paucity of information the whole question is obscure" {sfn|''JE''|1925}}In the early and middle 20th century, leading archaeologists such as [[William F Albright]], and biblical scholars such as [[Albrecht Alt]], believed that the patriarchs and matriarchs were either real individuals or believable composites of people who lived in the "[[patriarchal age]]", the 2nd millennium BCE In the 1970s, new arguments concerning Israel's past and the biblical texts challenged these views; these arguments can be found in [[Thomas L Thompson]]'s ''[[The Historicity of the Patriarchal Narratives]]'' (1974), and [[John Van Seters]]' ''[[Abraham in History and Tradition]]'' (1975) Thompson, a literary scholar, based his argument on archaeology and ancient texts His thesis centered on the lack of compelling evidence that the patriarchs lived in the 2nd millennium, and noted how certain biblical texts reflected first millennium conditions and concerns Van Seters examined the patriarchal stories and argued that their names, social milieu, and messages strongly suggested that they were [[Iron Age]] creations {sfn|Moore|Kelle|2011|p=18-19}} By the beginning of the 21st century, archaeologists had "given up hope of recovering any context that would make Abraham, Isaac or Jacob credible 'historical figures{{'"}} {sfn|Dever|2002|p=98 and fn
